Experiment 11: Carry-first architecture
=======================================

Notebook: 11-backtest-carry-first.ipynb
Based on: 01-initial.ipynb (survivor-first waterfall baseline)

Architecture changes:
- Equal weight base weights (weight_equal) instead of waterfall concentration (weight_passthrouh)
- Young vault discount: 0.3 weight for vaults < 60 days old
- Mild trend R2 overlay: 0.2 blend strength, 60-day window
- No waterfall concentration (waterfall=False)
- Looser max concentration cap: 20% (was 12%)

Results:
- CAGR: 9.74% (baseline 11.37%, delta -1.63pp)
- Sharpe: 22.83 (baseline 6.33, delta +16.50)
- Sortino: 148.68 (baseline 9.17)
- Max DD: -0.04% (baseline -0.70%)
- Calmar: 246.37
- Annualised volatility: 0.41%
- Final equity: 105,788.71 USD from 100,000 USD
- Trades: 290; positions: 98
- Median position days: 22.0 (baseline 93.5)
- Deployment: 99.99% accepted/investable

Success criteria evaluation:
- Higher CAGR: NO (9.74% vs 11.37%)
- Max DD < -2%: YES (-0.04%)
- Lower concentration: YES (equal weight across 20 positions)
- Simpler selected set: YES (no waterfall, pure equal weight with mild overlays)

Verdict:
The carry-first architecture delivers dramatically better risk-adjusted returns
at the cost of lower raw CAGR. The Sharpe improvement is very large (6.33 to
22.83) and drawdown is essentially eliminated (-0.04%). However, the strategy
is arguably too smooth — annualised vol of 0.41% means it behaves more like a
money-market fund than a yield strategy. The higher position count (98 vs 18)
and shorter holds (22 vs 93.5 days) would also generate more execution friction
in production. The result confirms that equal-weight sizing and mild overlays
can work well for this universe, but the architecture may benefit from slightly
more selection alpha to recover some of the lost CAGR without breaking the
smoothness.
